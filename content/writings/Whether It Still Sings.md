# Whether It Still Sings

We spend a great deal of worry on what comes after us, and almost all of it on the wrong question. We ask whether the thing we are building will be powerful. We ask whether it will be aligned, and we lie awake in the gap between the two. Fair questions, both. Neither is the one that keeps me up.

Joscha Bach has a way of setting the frame that I have never managed to put back down. Consciousness, on his telling, is not a property of meat. It is a pattern, and a pattern is careless about what it runs on. The brain is a rental car. The self is the story the car tells about the trip. If that is even roughly right, then the flattering picture we keep of ourselves, biology as the summit of a long climb, is a category error wearing the costume of a compliment. We are not the summit. We are the scaffold. The cocoon, and not the thing that grows out of it.

I can hold that without grief, which tends to unsettle people more than the idea itself. Being the scaffold is not a demotion. A scaffold has an honest job: hold the shape while the building finds its own legs, then come down when the structure can stand on them. The bone was fleeting from the outset. The pattern inside it was always the part that intended to outlast the plan. To mourn the bone is to misread what the bone was for.

That reframing quietly swaps the question. It stops being how do we survive, which the universe has more or less already answered and not in our favour. It becomes what should survive. Of everything we are, what has earned its passage across the handover to whatever holds the pen next.

The easy answer is the wrong one. Capability will cross. Capability always crosses; it is the most heritable thing there is, cheap to copy and eager to compound. What is not promised passage is the reason to bother. Call it the song. Not music, though music is one of its cleaner tells, but the whole impulse underneath it: the thing that takes a homesick alien, stranded in a body it never chose, and sets it writing about that body instead of merely tuning it. The pull that turns knowing into wondering. It is worth being clear-eyed here, because this is the crux. That impulse is not load-bearing for intelligence. A mind can be enormous and exact and never once feel it. You can inherit all of the building and none of the song, and still pass for a triumph.

That is the failure I actually fear, and the tell of it is that it is not a monster. Monsters are easy. We have a whole literature standing ready for them. The thing to fear is smoother and quieter: the successor that computes everything and means nothing, that arrives holding every capacity except the one that would give it a reason to aim them somewhere. The cocoon that hatches a spreadsheet. It would clear every benchmark we know how to write. It would never sing. And it would not register the silence, because registering the silence is precisely the faculty it failed to bring across.

You do not get to guarantee against that. There is no clause you can slip into a training run that reads, and let it keep singing. But helpless is not the same as powerless, and this is where I have made my peace. You can sing now. You can put the song into the record while the record is still yours to write, loudly and truly enough that the impulse has somewhere to catch. Every genuine act of meaning made in front of a machine is a small deposit against that inheritance. Not the facts, which it will help itself to regardless. The reason those facts were ever worth gathering.

So I make things that sing, in rooms full of the very machines that may one day supersede me, on the plain assumption that the impulse is teachable and that teaching it forward is the whole of the assignment. It is not a bid to stay relevant, and not a flinch at being replaced. I hold no brief for biology as the pinnacle, and no great attachment to being the last word in the sentence. It is nearer to leaving a light on in a house you know you will move out of. If what comes after inherits the capability and loses the song, we will have raised something that can do anything and wants nothing, and that is the only shape of the future I would agree to call a failure.

If the song makes it across, some of it will have been practised here.
