# If Ideas Are Illegible, Governance Fails

The smartest ideas go unheard. Not because they're wrong — because the format gets in the way.

I've spent 20+ years in extractive industries, ESG, development finance, and resettlement operations. In the last year I've also written 70+ newsletter episodes on AI accountability, built a 7-level AI-ESG governance curriculum, and developed voice agent prototypes for operational settings.

Every single one of those projects taught me the same thing: **the gap between expert knowledge and audience reception is where good work goes to die.**

The research exists. The frameworks exist. The audience — decision-makers, practitioners, boards, regulators — exists. What's missing is translation. Not dumbing down. Not "making it sexy." Translation that holds the complexity and still makes the ideas move.

---

## The Content Pathology Matrix

After watching hundreds of expert documents stall, I've identified five states of what I call draft purgatory:

**Too Dense** — Essential insights buried under cognitive load. Cross-functional stakeholders disengage before they reach the point.

**Too Messy** — The substance is present, but the lack of framing obscures the core value proposition. Nobody can find the argument.

**Too Jargon-Soaked** — Alienates the external audiences who most need to hear it. The expertise gets isolated in a silo of its own language.

**Too Cross-Domain** — Attempts to unify systems thinking across disciplines end up as disjointed fragments. The connections are real but the presentation breaks.

**"I know what I mean..."** — The most common one. The translation layer between SME intent and audience reception is broken. The author can see the through-line. Nobody else can.

Sound familiar?

---

## The Reframe

Complexity isn't the problem. The fields that need clarity most — AI governance, systems thinking, ESG, institutional design — are genuinely complex. Stripping that away isn't clarity. It's loss.

Standard content agencies flatten complexity into what I call corporate porridge: mushy material trying to speak to every audience at once. Bloated engagements, weeks of onboarding, generalized consultants struggling with highly specific concepts.

What's needed is the opposite: someone who can hold the complexity and still make the ideas move. Without flattening what makes them matter.
