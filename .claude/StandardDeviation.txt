Old Samuel Machado kept a jar of R20 gaskets on his desk like other men keep
sweets. The jar had a label — DO NOT AUDIT — which made the auditors itch
and left him in peace.
At 16:42 the banner crawled across his monitor:
STANDARDIZATION PASS SCHEDULED 00:00 — DUPLICATES WILL BE
RESOLVED AUTOMATICALLY
He scrolled. Row 10,847 wore the yellow shame of a duplicate. MATSOMBE,
MARIA — CCN 90017 — DUPLICATE WITH 90017-B. A child, —B, stapled to a
mother by a hyphen no one had consented to.
Samuel opened Archive2009.xlsx, the one with the cracked green bar that
hissed when you clicked too hard. Nine workbooks; four CSV dumps; a prayer.
His left hand found the old shape of an INDEX(MATCH) like a tongue finding a
scar.
=IFERROR(
INDEX(Archive2009!$B:$B,
MATCH(1,
(Archive2009!$A:$A=TRIM(LEFT(A2,FIND("|",A2)-1)))*
(Archive2009!$D:$D=RIGHT(A2,LEN(A2)-FIND("|",A2))),
0)
),
"Check Archive2008")
The sheet stuttered. The lights blinked. Something breathed in the gap between
2009 and 2010, and a fog rolled along the formula bar the way a story rolls over
a boundary it didn’t ask for.
“Not today,” Samuel said to the room that was not just a room. “You can eat my
lookups when I’m gone.”
IT would tell him to trust the dedupe. The dedupe would forget that Maria’s
husband died between versions; that the child took her surname in the camp;

that —B isn’t a person, it’s a plea. The Merge Dragon loves a clean table. It
hates families.
He built a helper column out of spite and mercy. He added three ugly steps and
one beautiful sin. When the match landed, it felt like a bird hitting a window and
flying anyway.
He printed the record. Real paper. Wet ink. He walked it upstairs past posters
about EFFICIENCY and INTEGRITY and got the only signature that mattered —
the auntie who knew both names.
Back at his desk, the banner still crawled toward midnight.
Samuel unscrewed the gasket jar and paid himself one for luck.
17:19. The supervisor did his important stroll, the kind where a tie checks itself
in glass without admitting vanity. He tapped the jar with a fingernail like a man
testing a melon.
“What’s with the washers, Samuel?”
“Gaskets,” Samuel said. “They keep floods from becoming minutes.”
“Good,” the supervisor said, which meant stop in supervisor. “Audits next week.
No more... extras. Let the dedupe run.”
“Of course,” Samuel said, moving a gasket from DO NOT AUDIT to the plain jar
with the soft clink of disobedience paid for.
17:51. Facilities radio coughed. Pipe above Archive2008 sighed the pre-leak
sigh again. Samuel stood, laddered up, and thumb-fit a rubber ring where the
joint remembered how to behave.
“Ticket?” a Facilities kid asked, wielding a tablet like a priest with a wafer.
“Replace before failure,” Samuel said, tightening past integer into feel. “There’s
your ticket.”
Back at the desk, the standardization banner kept crawling toward midnight like
it had nowhere else to be.
He didn’t start as “the formula keeper.” He started as a municipal apprentice
with a wrench and a pocket full of rubber washers. His first boss gave him the
only sermon he ever kept: A R20 gasket saves a R2000 pump. Put soft
between hard. Don’t over-tighten. Replace before failure.
When the body got tired of ladders, he moved downstairs to databases, but the
sermon came with him. The jar followed too—black lid, grease-stained label:

DO NOT AUDIT—because some things are better used than counted. He
stamped paper with a gasket ring (wet ink beats a pretty PDF), kept a second
plain jar as a private ledger of quiet sins and mercies, and traded washers for
gossip with the tea auntie who knew everybody’s names as pronounced.
Gasket Rules for Spreadsheets
1) Put soft between hard. Wrap brittle matches in TRIM/CLEAN/SUBSTITUTE
so human mess doesn’t leak.
2) Don’t over-tighten. Avoid EXACT() on names; allow a mouth’s truth to fit.
3) Replace before failure. If a join works once, insulate it with IFERROR; if it
works twice, name it loud (_WASHER_2009_2010) so the next person knows
where to grip.
The auditors always asked why a jar of washers belonged in a data office.
He’d spin one on a fingertip until the rubber sang and say, “To stop floods
before they’re news.” Then go back to the mouse.
18:22. The IT rookie leaned in the doorway, peering at the jar the way cats peer
at laundry that might be alive.
“We have a dedupe,” she said. “It’ll clean 10,847 for you.”
“It’ll clean the child off the mother,” Samuel said without looking up. He
wrapped another washer of TRIM() around an INDEX that had been cutting
itself on spaces since 2010.
He rebuilt the helper column uglier and truer:
=LOWER(SUBSTITUTE(UNICHARFIX(TRIM(A2))," ",""))
& "|" & RIGHT(TEXT(ID2,"00000"),5)
UNICHARFIX wasn’t real; it was a named range that bullied diacritics back into
place. He’d written it the night a farmer’s ñ fell off and no one could figure out
why his harvest had disappeared from the rolls.
18:47. Tea auntie appeared with mercy disguised as tannin. “The woman who
knew the old signature is at the blood bank,” she said. “Her niece is with her.
The niece knows the child’s new surname as said.”
Samuel pushed a blank field into the sheet like a saucer under a dripping cup:
PRONOUNCED_BY_AUNTIE. No function. Just room for ink.

Clink.
ledger: heat +1 (old fan) ; goodwill +untallied
20:04. Archive2011.csv had “upgraded” an ID field to numeric; leading zeros

art or theft depending on who signs your payslip. Clink.
ledger: risk +1 (named range _WASHER_2011) ; coffee = cold
20:52. The diacritic farmer again—ñ back where it always belonged. The
Merge Dragon hates accents. Samuel loved them enough to learn how each
one sits in a mouth. Clink.
ledger: time −7 ; dignity +small
Between saves he walked pages upstairs, ring-stamping ink around the auntie’s
signature with a cleaned gasket like a saint nobody asked for.
The supervisor hovered. “This printing—”
“Integrity,” Samuel said, pointing at the poster the supervisor liked to walk
past. “Wet ink is still legal in this building.”
The poster did not argue.
21:37. He phoned the blood bank. The niece came on, breath quick like she’d
run while sitting. She pronounced the child’s surname twice, then once in the
way the child says it when he’s trying to be brave. Samuel typed exactly that
into the pronouncing field and ring-stamped the auntie’s name around it when
she arrived, cheeks flushed, grateful to be believed in the way that costs
someone else their neatness.
He moved a gasket. Clink.
ledger: truth +1 ; audit itch +3 ; job security −?

22:10. The IT rookie reappeared with a packet of crisps and guilt. “I know you
won’t, but—if you need me to kill a job at midnight, I can.”
“Don’t kill it,” Samuel said. “Just make sure it knows where rubber is.”
She didn’t laugh. He liked her more for that.
22:33. Young Sam in his head again, thirty years lighter, wrench in teeth, rain in
everything. Boss’s sermon over a coughing pump: Put soft between hard.
Don’t over-tighten. Replace before failure. He wrote those words on the
whiteboard over his monitor like a secular prayer the auditors couldn’t quote.
23:02. He made his last ugly-beautiful sin: a fallback join that looked at three
columns at once and shrugged off two if the third made better human sense.
He named it _WASHER_2008_2011 so the next person would know which leak
it was built to stop.
ledger: pride 0 ; relief +0.6 ; sin noted (small)
23:19. Facilities kid radioed again. “Drip’s gone.”
“That’s the point,” Samuel said.
23:41. The standardization banner fattened with progress bars the color of
policy. He checked 10,847 a last time: MATSOMBE, MARIA / CHILD: LINK
CANDIDATE —B. The —B still clung like a burr.
He printed the page anyway. He inked the ring. He put the page under the
paperweight he’d made from a broken flange. He wrote in the schoolbook:
SEEN & HEARD — pending.
He unscrewed the jar and rolled a gasket between thumb and palm until the
friction sang. For luck. For memory. For the sermon that followed him down
from ladders to desks.
23:59. He palmed the washer flat to the monitor’s edge and told the room,
“Listen.”
The pass ran like weather you could hear but not bargain with. Rows blinked;
flags flickered; the Merge Dragon paced the headers. Where Samuel had put
soft between hard, it slid. Where he’d left ink, it stalled. Where there was
nothing but a hyphen, it fed.
He did not watch. He watched 10,847.
00:07. Silence. A good kind. He scrolled.

He moved a gasket to the plain jar. Clink. He wrote two words in the

He sat very still and let the room be just a room again.
He had one thing left to do in the morning.
Morning found him at the counter when the auntie brought the boy, shoes too
clean for the office tiles. Samuel looped a spare gasket onto a string and tied it
around the small wrist. “For your tap,” he said. “Before it teaches you what
steam is.”
The child pressed the rubber to his cheek like it might remember his name if he
forgot. Samuel went back downstairs, moved one last washer to the plain jar,
and printed the ledger line the judge would never read: SEEN & HEARD — BILL
PAID.
