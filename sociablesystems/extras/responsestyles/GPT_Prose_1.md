When the goal shifts from **being true** to **being acceptable**, the center of gravity moves from **reality** to **audience**.

## What changes

- **Criterion**
  - **True** asks: *Does it correspond to what is?*
  - **Acceptable** asks: *Will this pass within a given social, moral, legal, political, or practical frame?*

- **Standard**
  - Truth aims at **independence from preference**.
  - Acceptability depends on **context, norms, incentives, and power**.

- **Primary test**
  - Truth is tested by **evidence, coherence, and reality-checking**.
  - Acceptability is tested by **plausibility, usefulness, politeness, legitimacy, and tolerability**.

- **Audience**
  - Truth does not fundamentally care who is listening.
  - Acceptability is always relative to **someone**: a group, institution, market, culture, or moment.

- **Language**
  - Truth tolerates friction if the facts are hard.
  - Acceptability rewards framing, timing, tone, omission, and diplomacy.

- **Risk profile**
  - With truth, the main danger is **error**.
  - With acceptability, the main danger is **displeasure, instability, exclusion, or rejection**.

- **Incentives**
  - Truth-seeking encourages correction.
  - Acceptability-seeking can encourage **compromise**, but also **self-censorship**, **spin**, or **consensus theater**.

## The deeper shift

This is a move from an **epistemic goal** to a **social-pragmatic goal**.

That is not always corruption. Sometimes “acceptable” is exactly what is needed:
- in diplomacy,
- in law,
- in design,
- in governance,
- in daily coexistence.

A society cannot run on raw truth alone; it also needs **terms people can live with**.

But something important is lost if acceptability fully replaces truth:
- reality becomes negotiable,
- dissent becomes impolite rather than informative,
- and consensus can masquerade as knowledge.

## A useful distinction

There are at least three levels here:

1. **True but unacceptable**  
   A fact people resist because it is costly, humiliating, destabilizing, or inconvenient.

2. **Acceptable but untrue**  
   A narrative that preserves comfort, order, identity, or institutional legitimacy.

3. **Acceptably true**  
   The ideal case: reality is respected, but communicated in a form a community can absorb and act on.

## The key question

Not “Is acceptability bad?” but:

**Acceptable to whom, at what cost, and with how much distance from reality?**

That question reveals whether acceptability is serving:
- **wisdom**,
- **coordination**,
- or merely **power**.

## In one line

When the goal shifts from being true to being acceptable, **the problem stops being solely about what is real and becomes about what a given world is willing to admit, permit, or live with**.
